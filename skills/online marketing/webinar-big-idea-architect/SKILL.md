## Step 2: Get Input

Ask the user to provide the following (adapt based on any context already available from FutureProof):

1. **ICA Definition** — Who is the ideal audience for this webinar? What is their current situation, primary frustration, and aspirational identity? If an ICA profile exists in FutureProof context, confirm or refine it.
2. **Offer / Vehicle** — What product, programme, service, or method will the webinar ultimately pitch? What does it actually do and how does it work at a high level?
3. **Market Landscape** — Who are the 2–4 dominant competitors or alternative approaches the ICA is already aware of? What conventional wisdom does the ICA currently believe about solving their problem?
4. **Existing Assets** — Does the user have any prior webinar scripts, landing pages, VSLs, or campaign data they can share? Any proven proof elements (case studies, data points, testimonials)?
5. **Desired Outcome** — What is the single measurable business result this webinar must drive (e.g., application bookings, course enrolments, SaaS trial starts)?
6. **Constraints** — Webinar format (live, evergreen, hybrid), length target, and any brand or compliance guardrails.

If the user cannot answer all items, proceed with what is available and flag gaps as assumptions to validate.

## Step 3: Audit the Competitive Narrative Landscape

Before generating ideas, map the existing competitive terrain using the **Narrative Saturation Audit**:

1. **Dominant Claims Inventory** — List the top 5–7 promises and hooks competitors are currently running in ads, webinar registration pages, and sales pages targeting the same ICA.
2. **Conventional Wisdom Statement** — Articulate the prevailing belief the ICA holds about how to solve their problem (the "accepted truth" in the market).
3. **Mechanism Inventory** — Identify the mechanisms (named systems, frameworks, methods) competitors use to explain *why* their solution works.
4. **Saturation Score** — Rate each dominant claim on a 1–5 saturation scale (1 = novel, 5 = commoditised). Flag any claim scoring 4+ as a "dead zone" to avoid.

This audit ensures the Big Idea and Unique Mechanism are architected in genuine white space, not in a saturated narrative corridor.

## Step 4: Architect the Big Marketing Idea

Apply Todd Brown's E5 Method criteria to generate and evaluate candidate Big Marketing Ideas. A qualifying Big Marketing Idea must pass all five gates:

### Gate 1: Intellectually Interesting
The idea must provoke curiosity — it should make the ICA think, "I've never heard it explained that way before." Test: Would the ICA stop scrolling to read a headline built on this idea?

### Gate 2: Emotionally Compelling
The idea must connect to a deeply felt desire or fear. Map the idea to the ICA's **core emotional driver** (status, security, autonomy, belonging, or transformation).

### Gate 3: Uniquely Yours
The idea must be defensibly distinct — no other competitor could credibly claim the same positioning. Cross-reference against the Narrative Saturation Audit from Step 3.

### Gate 4: Believable on Its Face
The idea must not trigger immediate scepticism. It should feel bold but not absurd. Test: Does it require more than one sentence of justification before the ICA will entertain it?

### Gate 5: Delivers a Single, Clear "One Thing" (Brunson Criterion)
Per Russell Brunson's framework, the idea must collapse into one singular, unmistakable promise the entire webinar is built to prove. If the Big Idea requires a compound sentence to express, it is not yet refined.

**Process:**
- Generate 3–5 candidate Big Marketing Ideas using lateral thinking techniques (inversion, analogy transfer, reframing the enemy, elevating the stakes).
- Score each candidate against all five gates on a 1–10 scale.
- Select the highest-scoring candidate and refine it into a single declarative statement.

## Step 5: Define the Unique Mechanism

The Unique Mechanism is the *specific, named element* inside the user's offer that explains **why** the Big Idea is achievable and **why** previous approaches failed the ICA. It is the proprietary "engine" that makes the vehicle credible.

### 5A: Mechanism Type Classification

Determine which mechanism archetype applies:

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Process Mechanism** | A proprietary sequence or method | "The 4-Phase Recalibration Protocol" |
| **Component Mechanism** | A unique ingredient, element, or technology | "Our Cognitive Load Mapping Engine" |
| **Environmental Mechanism** | A unique context, container, or delivery model | "The 90-Day Immersion Ecosystem" |

### 5B: Mechanism Construction

Build the Unique Mechanism using the **3-Layer Articulation Framework**:

1. **Name It** — Create a proprietary, memorable name. It must sound credible (not gimmicky) and imply specificity. Use the format: [Modifier] + [Technical Noun] or [Number] + [Process Noun].
2. **Explain Why It Works** — Provide a 2–3 sentence mechanistic explanation that connects cause to effect. The ICA should understand the *logic* of why this mechanism produces results.
3. **Explain Why Nothing Else Has It** — Articulate the gap or flaw in every other approach that this mechanism corrects. This is the "reason competitors fail" narrative.

### 5C: Mechanism Validation Check

- Does the Unique Mechanism make the Big Idea **inevitable** rather than merely possible?
- Can the user substantiate the mechanism with at least one proof element (case study, data, demonstration)?
- Is the mechanism simple enough to explain in under 60 seconds during the webinar?

## Step 6: Formulate the One Big Promise

Synthesise the Big Marketing Idea and Unique Mechanism into a single **One Big Promise** statement using this formula:

> **"[ICA] can [specific, measurable transformation] by [timeframe/condition] through [Unique Mechanism], even if [primary objection neutralised]."**

Validate the One Big Promise against three criteria:

1. **Specificity** — Does it contain at least one concrete, quantifiable element (number, timeframe, measurable result)?
2. **Believability** — Does the inclusion of the Unique Mechanism make the promise feel *engineered* rather than aspirational?
3. **Objection Inoculation** — Does the "even if" clause pre-emptively neutralise the ICA's #1 reason for scepticism?

## Step 7: Deliver Output

Produce the **Webinar Strategic Foundation Document** containing the following sections:

### Section 1: ICA Snapshot
- ICA profile summary (confirmed or refined)
- Core emotional driver
- Conventional wisdom they currently hold

### Section 2: Narrative Saturation Audit
- Dominant claims table with saturation scores
- Identified white-space opportunities
- Dead zones to avoid

### Section 3: Big Marketing Idea
- Final Big Marketing Idea statement (1–2 sentences)
- Gate scorecard (scores across all five E5 gates)
- Runner-up ideas with rationale for rejection (retained for future testing)

### Section 4: Unique Mechanism
- Mechanism name
- Mechanism type classification
- 3-Layer Articulation (Name / Why It Works / Why Nothing Else Has It)
- Supporting proof element(s)

### Section 5: One Big Promise
- Final promise statement in the prescribed formula
- Validation against specificity, believability, and objection inoculation criteria

### Section 6: Strategic Implications for Scripting
- Recommended webinar title options (3 variants) built on the Big Idea
- Registration page headline candidates (3 variants)
- Opening hook direction — the first 90 seconds of the webinar mapped to the Big Idea
- Key belief shifts the webinar must engineer to make the One Big Promise land

### Section 7: Assumptions & Open Questions
- List of any assumptions made due to missing input
- Recommended validation steps before proceeding to full script development

Apply any user-specific `instructions` from FutureProof context to adjust tone, formatting, or strategic emphasis.
